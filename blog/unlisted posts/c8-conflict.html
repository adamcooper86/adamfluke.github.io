Responsible Conflict

How to figure out who should own a bad situation

Sunday May 31, 2015

Summary:

Conflict in work is inevitable, and all the more common in situations where someone is messing with your sandbox. How to prevent them, and resolve them when they go bad.

Who is going to take responsiblity for a difficult situation?

Recently, in a work situation at the school I worked at, there was a situation where it became clear that the support for a difficult student needed to change. This set off a cascade of difficult conversations where most were trying to pass-off the problem to someone else. There was a lot of he-said-she-said, a lot of confusion, and leadership. This impacted me personally, because I was being told that the whole situation was being passed to me, by coworkers, without talking to me about it, or deciding as a group.

Fuzzy Communication

At its core, this situation was a problem of communication. I was getting messages through other people, who themselves weren't involved in making decisions. This lead to me feeling disrespected, as I wasn't consulted about changes that would have a drastic impact on my classroom. I felt like I was being forced to take on a responsiblity, because others didn't want to take responsiblity. This made me think that they didn't respect me as a professional, and angry that others decisions were having a negative impact on my work.

Resolution

After a particularly heated discussion with a coworker, I insisted on bringing in our boss to resolve the conflict. I think this was suboptimal, as the final schedule of responsiblities could have been arranged by everyone without her help. However, in a situation like that, were the communication had broken down before it began, bringing in an arbiter is necessary. If I were the initiating party, I'd like to think that I would initiate a conversation with the other party, and will endevour to make it part of team culture in the teams I'm on in the future to talk to stake-holders directly.
